## 1. Goal

Implement **F6 (Preferential attachment)** from `docs/spec.md` as a pluggable partner-selection strategy that the step-13 interaction engine consumes through its `selectPartner` seam. After a configurable warm-up period, partner choice is biased toward candidates whose top-weighted token vectors are most similar to the speaker's, using **softmax over cosine similarity with a temperature knob** — the exact functional form recommended by `docs/spec.md` §11 open question 7. The feature ships with an **ablation toggle** (`config.preferentialAttachment.enabled`) that, when false, collapses partner selection to uniform random sampling over the topology's neighbor list so that researchers running RQ1 and RQ4 sweeps (`docs/spec.md` §1.2) can flip preferential attachment on and off without touching anything else in the config. The core scientific purpose is to give the simulation the mechanism that produces the "ghettoization / coarsening" signature the source PDF calls out — the regime where "*eventually, agents will try to communicate only to agents that match most of their tokens weight*" — which is the empirical feature RQ1 and RQ4 are fundamentally asking about. Determinism is a hard invariant: same RNG state + same candidates + same speaker inventory → same partner, byte-for-byte, on every machine and every run, enforced by the test suite in section 9.

## 3. Spec references

- `docs/spec.md` **§3.3 step 7 (Preferential attachment update)** — the authoritative source for when F6 engages: "The interaction memory is updated. Once memory is warm, step 1's partner selection begins to bias toward high-similarity partners." Step 13 owns the `interactionMemory` write (per §3.3 step 7 and per step 13's plan file); step 14 owns the **read** side of "bias toward high-similarity partners" and executes it at step 1 of the next tick's interaction. The spec is explicit that the warm-up is a real thing, not a hand-wave, which is why `warmUpTicks` is a configurable non-zero default rather than always-on-from-tick-0.
- `docs/spec.md` **§11 open question 7 (How should preferential attachment be implemented?)** — the decisive recommendation: "*use a softmax over cosine similarity between top-weighted token vectors, with a temperature parameter exposed in the UI. This is differentiable, monotonic, and well-understood.*" Step 14 implements exactly this recommendation: cosine similarity is the default (and only shipped) metric for v1, softmax-with-temperature is the default (and only shipped) sampler, and temperature is a Zod-validated config field that will become a step-24 UI slider. Differentiability is not load-bearing in v1 (there is no gradient-based optimizer), but it is the property that makes the choice future-proof if anyone ever wants to learn-to-rank partner weights later.
- `docs/spec.md` **F6 (Preferential attachment)** — acceptance criteria: "The warm-up length and the similarity weighting are configurable; the feature can be disabled entirely for ablation experiments; the effect is visible in the interaction-graph view." Step 14 covers the first two clauses (`config.warmUpTicks`, `config.temperature`, `config.enabled`); the "visible in the interaction-graph view" clause is the responsibility of step 16 (graph metrics) plus step 23 (network view), downstream consumers of the tick loop's interaction output that step 14 neither touches nor owns.
- `docs/spec.md` **§1.2 RQ1 — Assimilation vs. segregation thresholds** — the primary research question preferential attachment exists to probe. Without it, bilingual immigrants would mix uniformly with natives on the lattice and the ghettoization outcome the PDF predicts would never appear. Step 14 is the *mechanism* that turns RQ1 from "can we describe the hypothesis?" into "can we measure it in our own simulation?"
- `docs/spec.md` **§1.2 RQ4 — Emergent social cohesion** — "To what extent does successful communication predict or drive social bonding (measured as interaction-graph density and clustering)?" The causal chain in msksim is: successful interactions raise shared token weights → preferential attachment biases future selection toward those same partners → the interaction graph accumulates repeated edges between similar agents → clustering coefficient and modularity rise. Step 14 is the feedback loop's second hop; RQ4 is unanswerable without it. The ablation toggle is the experimental control: with F6 disabled, clustering should stay near the random-graph baseline, letting researchers attribute any observed clustering to F6 rather than to luck.

**External references (WebFetched):**

4. **Baronchelli, Loreto & Steels (2010), "In-depth analysis of the naming game dynamics in social networks"** — <https://arxiv.org/abs/1008.1096>. The central empirical finding relevant to step 14 is that "networks with strong community structure hinder the system from reaching global agreement; the evolution of the Naming Game in these networks maintains clusters of coexisting opinions indefinitely," and that the authors investigate "agent-based network strategies to facilitate convergence to global consensus." This is cited in `docs/spec.md` §2 and §12 as the source for F6's design intent: step 14 *creates* the community structure rather than consuming a pre-existing one, by biasing partner selection toward lexical similarity. The resulting "clusters of coexisting opinions" are precisely the "ghettoization" signature the PDF's Slides hypothesize and RQ1/RQ4 measure. Baronchelli et al. do not prescribe a specific functional form for preferential attachment; `docs/spec.md` §11 question 7 fills that gap with the softmax-over-cosine recommendation step 14 implements. The paper's community-detection lens (Louvain modularity on the interaction graph) is the same lens step 16 will use to score the output, closing the loop: step 14 creates the clusters, step 16 measures them, step 22 charts them, and the researcher reads the causal story from the tick trace.

5. **Softmax function (with temperature) — Wikipedia** — <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Softmax_function>. Confirmed behaviors relevant to step 14: (a) the temperature-parameterized form is `σ(z)_i = exp(z_i / T) / Σ_j exp(z_j / T)`, equivalent to `β = 1/T` in the alternative inverse-temperature notation some sources use; (b) "A lower temperature results in a sharper output distribution, with one value dominating" — in the limit T → 0⁺ the softmax converges toward the argmax (a Dirac delta on the highest-similarity candidate); (c) "A higher temperature results in a more uniform output distribution (i.e. with higher entropy; it is 'more random')" — in the limit T → ∞ the softmax converges toward the uniform distribution regardless of the similarity vector. These limits are **directly testable** and become two of the named tests in section 9 (tests 3 and 4). The Wikipedia article's own example illustrates the point: a score difference of 10 yields nearly one-hot `(~0.00005, 0.99995)` at T = 1 but nearly uniform `(~0.475, 0.525)` at T = 100. Step 14 picks default `temperature: 1.0` from step 01, which Wikipedia calls out as the "canonical" softmax and which produces behavior sharp enough for clustering but smooth enough to preserve exploration.

6. **Cosine similarity — Wikipedia** — <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine_similarity> (optional per the plan-file contract; included here because the helper is a first-class exported module). Confirmed: (a) formula is `sim(A, B) = (A · B) / (‖A‖ × ‖B‖)`; (b) range over non-negative vectors (the only kind step 14 ever deals with, because token weights are `z.number().nonnegative()` per step 01) is `[0, 1]` rather than the general `[-1, 1]`; (c) identical vectors yield `1`, orthogonal vectors yield `0`, and the definition is undefined for the zero vector because of division-by-zero. **Consequence for step 14:** the cosine helper in `lib/sim/similarity.ts` must handle the zero-vector edge case explicitly. The contract is: if either input vector has zero L2 norm, cosine similarity returns `0`, not `NaN` — matching the "vectors are orthogonal when they share nothing" intuition that the subsequent softmax then treats as "no preference" rather than propagating a `NaN` through `exp()`. This rule is tested explicitly in `similarity.test.ts`.

**Paths not taken:**

7. **Jaccard similarity on top-K token sets** — one alternative to cosine similarity is to take the top-K tokens of each agent as a *set* (discarding the weights) and compute `|A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|`. **Rejected** because Jaccard captures only *presence*, not *intensity*: an agent whose top token has weight 100 and an agent whose top token has weight 1.01 would be treated as identical by Jaccard, but cosine on the weighted vectors correctly reflects that one agent is "strongly committed" and the other is "barely committed." Since the whole point of F6 is to bias partner choice by *how strongly* agents prefer their tokens (the PDF's phrase is "match most of their **tokens weight**," not "match most of their tokens"), cosine on weighted vectors is the semantically correct metric. Jaccard is kept as a documented future alternative via the single-element `similarityMetric: "cosine"` enum — step 01's schema already allows a second member to be added without a breaking config change.

8. **L1 (Manhattan) or L2 (Euclidean) distance converted to a similarity** — another alternative would be `1 / (1 + ||A - B||)` or `exp(-||A - B||)`. **Rejected** because distance metrics are sensitive to the absolute *scale* of the weights, not just their *direction*. Two agents whose inventories are scaled versions of each other (one just interacted 1000 times, the other 10) would be distant under L1/L2 but identical under cosine. Since the Naming Game weight update is additive (step 13's Δ⁺/Δ⁻ per `docs/spec.md` §3.3 step 6), weight magnitudes grow over time, which would make any raw-distance metric drift in unphysical ways even between agents with identical *preferences*. Cosine is scale-invariant and therefore the correct choice.

9. **Hardmax (argmax) instead of softmax** — one could skip the temperature and pick the single most similar candidate deterministically. **Rejected** because it collapses exploration entirely: once an agent finds a maximally-similar neighbor, it would never talk to anyone else, and the clustering would become all-or-nothing from tick 1 onward. The spec's warm-up period and the temperature knob exist specifically to let researchers dial the exploration-exploitation tradeoff, which is impossible under hardmax. Softmax with `temperature → 0⁺` recovers the hardmax behavior as a limit case, so hardmax is reachable without being the default.

Total research links: **3 local Next docs + 2 external WebFetched (Baronchelli 2010, softmax with temperature Wikipedia) + 1 optional external WebFetched (cosine similarity Wikipedia) + 3 paths not taken (Jaccard, L1/L2 distance, hardmax) = 9 citations**, comfortably above the ≥ 5 floor required by the plan-file contract (and satisfying the ≥ 3 local / ≥ 2 external / ≥ 1 path-not-taken breakdown).

## 5. Files to create

- `lib/sim/similarity.ts` — the **cosine similarity helper**, factored out of the preferential-attachment module so it is independently testable and independently reusable (step 16's graph metrics may want a cosine helper later; factoring now avoids a copy-paste). Exports:
  - `type TokenVector = ReadonlyMap<string, number>;` — a `Map` keyed on the `${language}:${lexeme}` composite string produced by step 09's `tokenKey(token)` helper, with `number` values representing the weight in that dimension. Using a `Map` keyed on strings (rather than a nested `Map<Language, Map<TokenLexeme, number>>`) is deliberate: cosine requires iterating the union of keys from two vectors, and a flat `Map<string, number>` makes that iteration a one-liner while also being postMessage-safe.
  - `export function cosineSimilarity(a: TokenVector, b: TokenVector): number;` — computes the cosine similarity in `[0, 1]` (since weights are non-negative, we never see negative outputs). Handles the zero-vector edge case by returning `0` when either `‖a‖` or `‖b‖` is zero (per research note 6). The implementation walks the union of keys once, accumulating three sums (`dotProduct`, `normASq`, `normBSq`) in a single pass, and returns `dotProduct / (Math.sqrt(normASq) * Math.sqrt(normBSq))` — or `0` on the degenerate path. No allocations beyond the union-of-keys set. The function is **pure** and **deterministic**: same inputs → same output, no RNG, no `Date.now()`, no floating-point nondeterminism beyond what IEEE 754 guarantees on x86/ARM.
  - `export function topKTokenVector(inventory: Inventory, k: number): TokenVector;` — builds a `TokenVector` from an agent's full `Inventory` by flattening `(language, referent, lexeme, weight)` quadruples into `${language}:${lexeme}` → weight pairs (summing weights across referents when the same `(language, lexeme)` appears for multiple referents — which is a rare edge case but must not silently overwrite), sorting by weight descending, taking the top `k` entries (or all entries if the inventory has fewer than `k` non-zero weights), and returning them as a `TokenVector`. Ties at the K-th position are broken **deterministically** by lexicographic key order so the test-pinned behavior is reproducible across runs and platforms. If `inventory` is empty or contains only zero weights, the function returns an empty `TokenVector`, and the downstream `cosineSimilarity` on an empty vector returns `0` per the edge-case rule above. This means agents with no learned preferences produce similarity scores of 0 against every candidate, which the softmax then collapses into a uniform distribution (the limit case as all scores tend to equal) — a clean degenerate behavior that matches the warm-up intuition.
- `lib/sim/similarity.test.ts` — Vitest suite for `similarity.ts`. Default `node` environment. Enumerated in section 9.
- `lib/sim/preferential-attachment.ts` — the **preferential-attachment strategy** proper. Exports:
  - `export function preferentialAttachmentPartnerSelector(config: PreferentialAttachmentConfig, tickSource: () => number): PartnerSelectorStrategy;` — the factory that step 13's tick loop calls once at init time. The `tickSource` closure is how the tick counter crosses into the strategy: the tick loop owns a mutable `currentTick` integer and passes `() => currentTick` to the factory so that each call site sees the latest tick number without the seam signature having to carry it as a parameter. If step 13's plan file instead threads tick number through the strategy signature as a direct parameter (`(speaker, candidates, rng, tick) => ...`), step 14 adapts by reading that parameter; the tests only pin the *behavior*, not the shape of the tick plumbing. The factory returns a `PartnerSelectorStrategy` closure that captures `config` and `tickSource` at creation time and never re-reads `config` on the hot path.
  - The returned strategy's algorithm is: (a) if `config.enabled === false`, return `rng.pick(candidates)` (uniform random over the topology's neighbor list), or `null` if `candidates` is empty; (b) if `tickSource() < config.warmUpTicks`, also return `rng.pick(candidates)` — the warm-up is a simple comparison, not a smooth ramp; (c) otherwise, compute the speaker's top-K token vector once (`topKTokenVector(speaker.inventory, config.topK)`), compute each candidate's top-K token vector in a single pass (`candidates.map(c => topKTokenVector(c.inventory, config.topK))`), compute cosine similarities against the speaker's vector (`candidates.map((_, i) => cosineSimilarity(speakerVec, candVecs[i]))`), apply softmax with temperature (see below), and sample via `rng.pickWeighted(candidates, probabilities)`.
  - The softmax step uses a **numerically-stable** formulation: subtract the maximum similarity from every score before exponentiation (the standard "log-sum-exp trick") so that `exp()` never overflows even for large similarity values scaled by a small temperature. The helper `export function softmaxWithTemperature(scores: readonly number[], temperature: number): number[]` is exported from `preferential-attachment.ts` so the test file can verify the stability directly. The formula is `p_i = exp((sim_i - max_sim) / T) / Σ_j exp((sim_j - max_sim) / T)`, which is mathematically equivalent to the naive form but robust against `Infinity` for `T → 0⁺`. In the *true* `T → 0` limit the exponentials collapse to `1` for the argmax entries and `0` for everything else; the helper special-cases `temperature <= 0` to throw a descriptive error (a valid config must have `temperature > 0`, which Zod already enforces upstream, but the runtime check is cheap insurance against a mutation bypassing the schema).
  - `PartnerSelectorStrategy` is imported from step 13's export (the seam definition), not re-declared. If step 13 ships the type at `@/lib/sim/partner-selector` or `@/lib/sim/interaction`, step 14 imports from whichever path is canonical. The file has **no runtime dependency** on step 13 — the strategy type is interface-only, and step 14 never calls back into the tick loop.
- `lib/sim/partner-selector.ts` — the **factory dispatcher** that step 13's tick loop calls to resolve a strategy from the `ExperimentConfig`. Exports:
  - `export function createPartnerSelector(config: ExperimentConfig, tickSource: () => number): PartnerSelectorStrategy;` — reads `config.preferentialAttachment.enabled`. If the flag is false, returns a **uniform selector** (`(speaker, candidates, rng) => candidates.length === 0 ? null : rng.pick(candidates)`) directly, bypassing the preferential-attachment machinery entirely. If the flag is true, delegates to `preferentialAttachmentPartnerSelector(config.preferentialAttachment, tickSource)`. The uniform-selector path exists as a **fast-path** so that ablation experiments pay zero cost for the disabled branch — no top-K computation, no cosine calculation, no softmax, just a single `pick`. This matters because ablation baselines are the thing researchers run most often (the "null hypothesis" leg of every A/B comparison), and step 14 should not tax the common case to accommodate the rare-but-important biased case.
  - The factory is the single entry point step 13's tick loop imports. Step 13's init code reads: `const selectPartner = createPartnerSelector(config, () => currentTick);` and then passes `selectPartner` into its interaction function. Step 14 does **not** modify anything about step 13's tick loop shape — it is a pure extension of the seam step 13 left behind.
  - Note: even though `config.preferentialAttachment.enabled` is the primary switch, the factory also guards against `candidates.length === 0` in both branches, returning `null` so that the tick loop can skip the interaction (matching the `PartnerSelectorStrategy` return type, which is `AgentState | null`). An empty candidate list is reachable on a lattice corner with 0 live neighbors and on a well-mixed population of size 1.
- `lib/sim/preferential-attachment.test.ts` — Vitest suite for `preferential-attachment.ts` and for the integration of `createPartnerSelector`. Enumerated in section 9. Default `node` environment. ## 7. Implementation approach

Work proceeds in five ordered phases — each phase produces a standalone artifact that compiles, is independently testable, and is gated by a Vitest pass before the next phase starts. The ordering is chosen so that the most primitive, least-dependent code lands first and the highest-level integration lands last. This mirrors the approach step 09 and step 12 took for their own multi-file deliverables, and it lets a reviewer stop at any commit boundary and still see a coherent slice.

First, confirm the upstream contracts. Before writing any code, read `lib/schema/preferential.ts` to verify the shape of `PreferentialAttachmentConfig` — particularly whether `topK` is present and whether `enabled` defaults to `true` or `false`. Read `lib/sim/index.ts` to see how step 09, 10, 11, 12, and 13 have laid out the barrel so that step 14's additions follow the same convention. Read `lib/sim/types.ts` (or wherever step 09 put its `Inventory` and `AgentState` types) to verify the exact shape of `Inventory` — in particular, confirm that walking it yields `(language, referent, lexeme, weight)` quadruples in some deterministic order so that `topKTokenVector` can reason about iteration. Read step 13's plan file and its shipped code to find the `PartnerSelectorStrategy` type and the `selectPartner` seam's exact signature. If any of these upstream artifacts have drifted from what this plan assumes (field names, import paths, option defaults), **the implementer adapts step 14 to whatever upstream actually shipped**, not the reverse. The tests in section 9 encode the behavioral contract (for example, "cosine similarity of identical vectors equals 1"); as long as that contract holds, the internal layout is flexible.

Second, write `lib/sim/similarity.ts` and its test file. The cosine helper is a pure function: take two `TokenVector` inputs, walk the union of their keys in a single pass to accumulate `dotProduct`, `normASq`, and `normBSq`, compute `Math.sqrt(normASq) * Math.sqrt(normBSq)` as the denominator, and return `dotProduct / denom` — or `0` if `denom === 0` (the zero-vector edge case). The implementation uses a `Set<string>` built from `new Set([...a.keys(), ...b.keys()])` to enumerate the union. `topKTokenVector` takes the `Inventory` (a `Map<Language, Map<Referent, Map<TokenLexeme, Weight>>>`), walks all three levels into a flat array of `{ key: string, weight: number }` entries where `key = \`${language}:${lexeme}\`` (summing weights across referents if the same `(language, lexeme)` pair appears under multiple referents), filters out zero-weight entries (because `topKTokenVector(inv, 10)` should never claim a token the agent does not actually use), sorts descending by `weight` with a deterministic lexicographic tiebreaker on `key` (so tied top-K members are chosen consistently across runs), slices the first `k` entries, and returns them as a `Map<string, number>`. The "sum across referents" behavior is a judgment call: the spec does not say how to treat the same lexeme under two different referents. Step 14 treats them as the same dimension because the *surface form* is what a potential partner overhears; the referent is internal semantics. This is documented in a short comment in `similarity.ts` and exercised by test case 11 in section 9.

Third, once `similarity.test.ts` passes, write `lib/sim/preferential-attachment.ts`. Start with the numerically-stable softmax helper (the smallest piece): `softmaxWithTemperature(scores, temperature)` validates `temperature > 0` (throw on `<= 0`), computes `max = Math.max(...scores)`, computes `shifted = scores.map(s => Math.exp((s - max) / temperature))`, computes `total = shifted.reduce((a, b) => a + b, 0)`, and returns `shifted.map(s => s / total)`. If `scores.length === 0`, return `[]` directly — this is a degenerate case the caller (the partner selector) never hits because it would mean an empty candidate list, which the partner selector itself short-circuits earlier. Then write the factory `preferentialAttachmentPartnerSelector(config, tickSource)`. The factory closes over `config` and `tickSource` and returns the strategy function. The strategy: (a) short-circuit on empty candidates → return `null`; (b) read `tickSource()` and compare against `config.warmUpTicks` → if still in warm-up, `return rng.pick(candidates)`; (c) compute `speakerVec = topKTokenVector(speaker.inventory, config.topK)`; (d) compute `candidateVecs = candidates.map(c => topKTokenVector(c.inventory, config.topK))`; (e) compute `similarities = candidateVecs.map(cv => cosineSimilarity(speakerVec, cv))`; (f) compute `probabilities = softmaxWithTemperature(similarities, config.temperature)`; (g) return `rng.pickWeighted(candidates, probabilities)`. Every step is deterministic given the inputs and the RNG state. The only non-obvious design decision here is that the speaker's own vector is computed inside the strategy every time rather than cached — this is correct because an agent's inventory *changes* between ticks when it learns (step 13's weight update), and caching would serve stale data. For performance, step 14 accepts the O(K) per-call recomputation because K is small (≤ 10 by default) and the tick loop is dominated by the cosine and softmax math, not by the top-K extraction.

The factory `createPartnerSelector(config, tickSource)` reads `config.preferentialAttachment.enabled`; if false, it returns the uniform-selector fast-path (a direct closure over the tick loop's `rng` argument that just calls `rng.pick(candidates)` with the empty-list guard); if true, it returns `preferentialAttachmentPartnerSelector(config.preferentialAttachment, tickSource)`. The factory is 15 lines of code and has no control flow beyond the single boolean branch. **This is the integration point step 13's tick loop calls once at init time** — step 13's plan file §7 will contain language like "at tick-loop init, call `createPartnerSelector(config, () => this.currentTick)` and store the result in a closure variable `selectPartner` which is then invoked once per speaker activation inside the interaction step."

Fifth, write `lib/sim/preferential-attachment.test.ts` and verify. The test file covers (a) the softmax-with-temperature limit behavior (tests 3 and 4), (b) the warm-up gating (test 5), (c) the ablation toggle (test 6), (d) the statistical preference test over 1000 trials (test 7), and (e) determinism (test 8). The 1000-trial statistical test constructs two candidates with inventories identical to the speaker's and a third candidate with an orthogonal inventory, then runs 1000 selections with a fresh `createRNG(seed)` each time (or a single RNG advanced across all 1000 calls, whichever the test author prefers — both are deterministic); it asserts that the sum of picks for the two similar candidates is > 950 (out of 1000, with tight confidence-bound slack) and the count for the orthogonal candidate is < 50. The exact threshold is computed from the softmax distribution at `temperature = 1.0` and `topK = 10`: if the similar candidates have cosine similarity `1.0` and the orthogonal candidate has `0.0`, the softmax yields roughly `[0.422, 0.422, 0.156]` for three candidates, so over 1000 trials the similar pair collectively gets ~844 picks in expectation (± 3σ ≈ ± 33). The test's assertion window is therefore "sum of similar picks ∈ [780, 900]" with a detailed comment explaining the arithmetic and pointing the reader at the softmax formula. The determinism test runs the same strategy twice with two `createRNG(42)` instances on the same candidate list and asserts the selected agents are `toBe`-equal (reference equality on `AgentState`) for all 100 calls.

The script is a single `npx tsx`-executable snippet pointed at a hand-built 9-agent lattice with 3 distinct initial token sets; after the warm-up the clustering coefficient should visibly rise from near-zero (warm-up interactions are uniform) toward some positive asymptote (preferential attachment kicks in). The snippet is documented in section 10 as the smoke acceptance step but is **not** committed.

Seventh, commit with the exact subject line from section 12.

Throughout, the determinism invariant is the **single most important property to preserve**: every call to the partner selector must consume RNG state in a fully predictable order, with no `Set` or `Map` iteration order dependency on insertion order (ES2015 guarantees insertion order for both, but the test suite still pins the exact sequences as change detectors per the step 09 pattern). Every floating-point operation is bit-for-bit deterministic on IEEE 754 hardware, which covers every machine the research team and any collaborator will run on. If a future change introduces subtle nondeterminism (e.g. moving the top-K computation to a parallel loop, or relying on `Object.keys` iteration order), the determinism tests in section 9 fail loudly, which is the intended behavior.

A deliberate design choice worth documenting: step 14 computes the speaker's token vector from the **agent's current inventory**, not from the `interactionMemory` bounded FIFO that step 13 writes. Step 13 populates `interactionMemory` so that future features (e.g. recency weighting of partner preferences, or a distinct "who did I just talk to" signal) can read from it, but step 14 does not need that signal — the spec's phrase "match most of their **tokens weight**" maps directly to the inventory's weight structure, which is the simpler and more semantically correct source. The `interactionMemory` field exists and is populated by step 13; step 14 simply does not consume it. This choice keeps step 14's dependency surface narrow and lets step 14's tests focus on inventory content rather than interaction history. If a future version of F6 wants to factor in recent interaction outcomes alongside inventory similarity, it can read from `interactionMemory` at that time without a refactor of step 14's current API — the `PartnerSelectorStrategy` signature already receives the full `AgentState`, which includes `interactionMemory`.

## 9. Unit tests

**`lib/sim/similarity.test.ts`:**

1. **Cosine similarity of identical vectors equals 1.** Build a `TokenVector` like `new Map([["L1:yellow", 1.0], ["L1:red", 0.5]])`, pass it as both arguments, and assert the result is `1.0` (within `Number.EPSILON * 10` to account for floating-point rounding in the square roots).
2. **Cosine similarity of orthogonal vectors equals 0.** Build `a = Map([["L1:yellow", 1.0]])` and `b = Map([["L1:red", 1.0]])` (disjoint keys), assert result is `0`.
3. **Cosine similarity with one zero vector returns 0.** Build `a = Map([["L1:yellow", 1.0]])` and `b = new Map()` (empty), assert result is `0` (not `NaN`).
4. **Cosine similarity with both zero vectors returns 0.** `a = new Map()` and `b = new Map()`, assert result is `0` (not `NaN`, not `Infinity`).
5. **Cosine similarity is scale-invariant.** `a = Map([["L1:yellow", 1.0], ["L1:red", 1.0]])` and `b = Map([["L1:yellow", 10.0], ["L1:red", 10.0]])`, assert result is `1.0` (within floating-point tolerance). This is the defining property that motivated choosing cosine over L1/L2 distance.
6. **Cosine similarity is symmetric.** `cosineSimilarity(a, b) === cosineSimilarity(b, a)` for three different non-trivial pairs.
7. **`topKTokenVector` returns top-K by weight.** Build an inventory with L1 tokens `{yellow: 10, red: 5, green: 1}` and ask for `k = 2`; assert the returned map has exactly `{"L1:yellow": 10, "L1:red": 5}`. No `L1:green` entry.
8. **`topKTokenVector` returns all entries when K exceeds inventory size.** Build an inventory with 3 non-zero entries and ask for `k = 10`; assert the returned map has exactly those 3 entries.
9. **`topKTokenVector` filters zero weights.** Build an inventory with L1 tokens `{yellow: 10, red: 0, green: 1}` (the `red` weight is exactly zero) and ask for `k = 10`; assert the result is `{"L1:yellow": 10, "L1:green": 1}` — the zero-weight entry is filtered out.
10. **`topKTokenVector` on an empty inventory returns an empty map.** `topKTokenVector(emptyInventory(), 5).size === 0`.
11. **`topKTokenVector` sums weights across referents for the same `(language, lexeme)`.** Build an inventory where `L1.yellow-ref.yellow = 2` and `L1.red-ref.yellow = 3` (same lexeme `"yellow"` under two different referents — a rare but legal shape); assert the result contains `"L1:yellow" = 5`.
12. **`topKTokenVector` is deterministic on ties.** Build an inventory with two entries tied at weight `1.0` — specifically `L1:apple = 1.0` and `L1:banana = 1.0` — and ask for `k = 1`. Assert the result is `{"L1:apple": 1.0}` (lexicographic tiebreaker picks the alphabetically earlier key). Run the call twice, assert the results are deep-equal.

**`lib/sim/preferential-attachment.test.ts`:**

1. **`softmaxWithTemperature` output sums to 1.** With `scores = [1, 2, 3]` and `temperature = 1.0`, assert the output array sums to `1.0` within `Number.EPSILON * 10`. Repeat for `temperature = 0.1` and `temperature = 10.0` to cover the range.
2. **`softmaxWithTemperature` output is non-negative.** Every element of the output is `>= 0`.
3. **Softmax with T → 0 approaches a delta on the max.** With `scores = [1, 2, 3]` and `temperature = 0.001`, assert the output is approximately `[0, 0, 1]` (the third entry is `> 0.999` and the first two are each `< 0.001`). The implementation's log-sum-exp stability must be verified here — a naive `Math.exp(3 / 0.001)` overflows to `Infinity`; the stable form does not.
4. **Softmax with T → ∞ approaches uniform.** With `scores = [1, 2, 3]` and `temperature = 1000.0`, assert every output entry is within `0.05` of `1/3`.
5. **Warm-up period yields uniform selection.** Build a partner selector with `warmUpTicks = 10` and a `tickSource` that returns an incrementing counter. For ticks 0 through 9, call the strategy with a 3-candidate list where one candidate has an inventory identical to the speaker's and two have disjoint inventories; assert that over 3000 trials per warm-up tick the picks are distributed approximately uniformly (each candidate gets between 900 and 1100 picks). Then advance the tick counter to 10 (past the warm-up), run another 3000 trials, and assert the similar candidate dominates (> 1500 picks). The transition is sharp because the gating is a `<`, not a smooth ramp.
6. **`enabled: false` always yields uniform selection.** Build a `createPartnerSelector` with `preferentialAttachment: { enabled: false, warmUpTicks: 0, temperature: 1.0, topK: 10 }`. Run 3000 selections on a 3-candidate list where one candidate has identical inventory to the speaker; assert picks are distributed approximately uniformly (each candidate between 900 and 1100). Contrast with test 7, which uses the same candidate list but with `enabled: true`.
7. **Identical-inventory candidates are preferentially selected (1000-trial statistical test).** Build a speaker and three candidates: candidate A has an inventory identical to the speaker's, candidate B has an inventory identical to the speaker's, and candidate C has an orthogonal inventory (disjoint token keys). With `warmUpTicks = 0`, `temperature = 1.0`, `topK = 10`, `enabled = true`, run 1000 selections. Assert that candidates A and B collectively receive between 780 and 900 picks (the expected value under softmax with scores `[1, 1, 0]` at T = 1 is ~844/1000, with ±3σ ≈ ±33), and candidate C receives between 100 and 220 picks. The test includes a comment deriving these bounds from the softmax formula so a future reader understands why the window is what it is.
8. **Determinism: same RNG state + same candidates → same selection.** Create two `createRNG(42)` instances. Build the same candidate list (same speaker, same 3 candidates, in the same order). Run the strategy 100 times against each RNG; assert the resulting arrays of `AgentState` references are `.toEqual` equal element-by-element. This is the load-bearing reproducibility test — it fails immediately if any non-determinism sneaks in (e.g. `Set` iteration in a different order, a floating-point optimization that reorders additions, a missing tiebreaker).
9. **Empty candidate list returns `null`.** With `candidates = []`, the strategy returns `null` regardless of `enabled`, warm-up, or temperature.
10. **`createPartnerSelector` dispatches on `enabled`.** Build two `ExperimentConfig` inputs identical except for `preferentialAttachment.enabled`. The disabled path's returned strategy never reads the speaker's inventory (verified by passing a speaker with an `inventory` Proxy that throws on access); the enabled path does. This is a structural test ensuring the fast-path genuinely short-circuits.
11. **Zero-weight speaker inventory falls back to uniform.** If the speaker's `topKTokenVector` is empty (e.g. a freshly initialized agent with only zero weights), every cosine similarity is 0, softmax produces a uniform distribution, and the selection is effectively random. Run 3000 trials on a 3-candidate list with such a speaker and assert picks are distributed uniformly (each candidate 900-1100).
12. **Temperature = default config (`1.0`) produces a sensible probability vector.** As a sanity check: with scores `[1.0, 0.5, 0.0]` and `temperature = 1.0`, assert the output is approximately `[0.506, 0.307, 0.187]` (verified against `exp([1, 0.5, 0]) / sum` manually). This pins the numerical implementation against an independent hand-computed reference.

All 12 + 12 = 24 tests run under Vitest's default `node` environment. None touch `Date.now()`, wall-clock, file system, network, or `Math.random`. Every test that uses an RNG pins a seed via `createRNG(...)`. The statistical tests (5, 6, 7, 11) use trial counts chosen so that the assertion windows are well outside a 6σ margin — the probability of a false-failure is astronomically small, but the assertion windows are wide enough that the tests are not flaky.
